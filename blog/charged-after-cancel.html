Charged After Cancelling a Subscription? Here's What Actually Works

A practical guide for Strava, Dropbox, Calm, Evernote, and Headspace — why the charge happened anyway, the exact steps to actually stop it, and what to do if you've already been billed.

Updated July 2026

I got a renewal charge last month for something I was pretty sure I'd already cancelled. Went to check, and sure enough — I'd cancelled it on the website, but I'd originally signed up through the App Store, so the website cancellation didn't touch the actual billing at all. Two different systems, only one of which I'd actually talked to.

That sent me down a rabbit hole of exactly how often this happens to other people, and it turns out it's extremely common. A survey by CableTV.com (via Pollfish, ~1,000 US adults, early 2025) found that 48% of Americans have forgotten to cancel a free trial and been charged for it at least once — basically a coin flip. It's common enough that the FTC introduced its "Click to Cancel" rule, which requires cancelling a subscription to be at least as easy as signing up for one, and in 2025 the FTC took enforcement action against Uber over deceptive billing and cancellation practices with Uber One. This isn't a "you weren't careful enough" problem — it's a "the billing system was built this way" problem. Companies know it's common enough that even Netflix has a help-center page literally titled "Charged after canceling Netflix."

Below are the five services people search for most on exactly this problem, with what actually causes it and the concrete steps that get it done — verified against each company's own help center.

Strava

Why it happens: Strava's subscription billing is owned by whichever platform you originally paid through — the website, Apple, or Google Play — and it keeps running through that platform even if you think you cancelled "in the app."

How to actually cancel it

Web: Settings → My Account → Cancel Subscription.
iOS: in the app, tap the You tab → gear icon → Your Strava Subscription → Manage Your Subscription — or go straight to Settings → your name → Subscriptions on the device, since Apple owns that billing relationship, not Strava.
Android: has to be cancelled through Google Play → Subscriptions, not from inside the Strava app itself.

Cancelling doesn't touch your activity history — you just lose the paid features once the current billing term ends.

If you've already been charged: Strava support does refunds within 14 days of the purchase date. Past 14 days, charges are stated as non-refundable, but it's still worth asking — worst case they say no.

Dropbox

Why it happens: Dropbox's own help center is upfront that a cancellation "may not be immediately reflected" on your account — your plan stays active and you're not charged again only once the current billing cycle ends. If a cancellation request arrives after that cycle's charge has already gone out (this comes up especially with cancellations submitted by physical mail), that charge isn't refunded.

How to actually cancel it

Web (dropbox.com): log in, click your avatar in the bottom-left corner → Manage account → Cancel plan at the bottom of the page → pick a reason → confirm.
Android: open the Dropbox app → Account → Manage your subscription → How to cancel → Cancel plan, which hands off to Google Play → Cancel subscription.
iOS: Settings → your name → Subscriptions → Dropbox → Cancel Subscription.

Either way your account drops to the free Dropbox Basic plan at the end of the current billing cycle, and Dropbox keeps deleted file versions for 30 days in case you change your mind.

If you've already been charged: Dropbox's stated policy is that subscription payments generally aren't refundable, with one carve-out — customers in the EU, UK, or Turkey get a 14-day refund window from the purchase date. If you subscribed through the Apple App Store or Google Play, Dropbox can't issue that refund at all — you have to request it from Apple or Google directly, since they hold the payment. Failing that, a bank chargeback will force the downgrade to the free plan.

Calm

Why it happens: Calm's cancellation flow is notorious for not "counting" unless you click all the way through to a final confirmation screen — plus the same platform-mismatch problem as the others: uninstalling the app does nothing to the underlying billing.

How to actually cancel it

Calm.com (web): log in at calm.com/profile → Settings → Manage Subscription → Cancel Subscription, and make sure you reach the final confirmation screen — it doesn't count until you see it.
Android: open Play Store → profile icon → Payments & subscriptions → Subscriptions → Calm → Cancel Subscription.
Apple: Calm can't see or cancel an Apple-billed subscription from their side — go to Settings → your name → Subscriptions → Calm → Cancel.
Cancel at least 24 hours before your renewal date. Once the charge has gone through, you're into refund-request territory instead of cancellation.

If you've already been charged: for Apple-billed subscriptions, use Apple's "Report a Problem" page to request a refund directly from Apple. For web-billed subscriptions, Calm has a published Refund Policy you can request against through their support.

Evernote

Why it happens: the exact cancellation path depends on whether you're billed directly by Evernote, through the App Store, or through Google Play — people often cancel in the wrong place, or expect the account to change instantly when it actually just stops renewing at the end of the current period.

How to actually cancel it

Evernote's own help article covers all three billing paths and asks you to pick the one that matches how you originally subscribed — trying to guess the flow without checking which platform actually billed you is the most common way this goes wrong.

Your account doesn't get deleted or downgraded immediately when you cancel — it stays on Premium/Pro until the end of the current billing period, then drops to Evernote Free automatically. You should get a confirmation email; if you don't, check Account Settings → Billing History — a "Restore" button next to your plan there confirms the cancellation actually took and it won't renew.

If you've already been charged: if support isn't responding after you flag it, that's a chargeback-with-your-bank situation rather than something to keep waiting on.

Headspace

Why it happens: the cancel option reportedly goes missing for some accounts on headspace.com, and if you're on the Headspace+Spotify bundle, the subscription is actually managed on Spotify's side, not Headspace's — cancelling in Headspace's own settings does nothing for that bundle.

How to actually cancel it

Subscribed directly at headspace.com: go to headspace.com/subscription/manage and click "Cancel Membership." If that option isn't there for your account, email help@headspace.com directly — support says they'll do it manually in that case.
Apple: Settings → your name → Subscriptions → Headspace → Cancel Subscription.
Google Play: Play Store → Menu → Subscriptions → Headspace → Cancel.
Headspace+Spotify bundle: managed entirely through Spotify — go to spotify.com/account/subscription and cancel from the Subscription section there.

If you've already been charged: subscriptions bought directly through headspace.com are listed as non-refundable by default, but Headspace says they'll review individual cases if you email support and explain what happened.

The pattern across all of them

Every one of these has the same two root causes: cancelling doesn't retroactively stop a charge that's already in-flight for the current billing cycle, and cancelling in the wrong place — the company's website when you actually paid via Apple or Google Play, or vice versa — does absolutely nothing to the billing that's actually running. It's not one company being careless; it's the default shape of how subscription billing works almost everywhere.
